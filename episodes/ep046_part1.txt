Hey everyone, welcome back to AI Dev Tools — the Crazyrouter Podcast. Today we're talking about something that affects every developer building with generative AI: content labeling. The era of "is this AI or human?" is over. Platforms are now requiring you to answer that question programmatically.

Let's break down what's happening and what it means for your code.

The trigger this week: Spotify is rolling out verified human badges for artists to distinguish them from AI-generated music. YouTube already requires creators to disclose AI-generated content. Meta labels AI images across Instagram and Facebook. And the EU AI Act's transparency requirements kick in for general-purpose AI systems this year.

This isn't a niche policy discussion. If you're building anything that generates text, images, audio, or video through an API, content labeling is becoming a technical requirement, not just a nice-to-have.

Let's talk about the three layers of AI content identification that developers need to understand.

Layer one: metadata. The C2PA standard — that's the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity — is becoming the de facto way to embed origin information into files. OpenAI already attaches C2PA metadata to images generated by DALL-E and GPT-image-2. Google does the same with Imagen. When you generate an image through an API, that metadata travels with the file. The problem? Most platforms strip metadata on upload. So metadata alone isn't enough, but it's the foundation.

Layer two: invisible watermarks. This is where it gets technically interesting. OpenAI embeds invisible watermarks in GPT-image-2 outputs. Google's SynthID watermarks text, images, audio, and video from their models. These watermarks survive screenshots, compression, cropping, and format conversion — at least in theory. The detection isn't perfect, but it's getting better fast. As a developer, you generally don't need to add these yourself — the model providers handle it. But you do need to know they're there, especially if your pipeline modifies generated content in ways that might strip or corrupt watermarks.

Layer three: platform-level disclosure. This is the one that hits your product directly. YouTube, TikTok, Spotify, and Meta all now have disclosure requirements for AI-generated content. If your app generates content that users post to these platforms, you need to either label it automatically or make it easy for users to self-disclose. Some platforms are building detection into their upload pipelines. Others rely on creator honesty. Either way, if your tool generates the content, you're part of the chain.
