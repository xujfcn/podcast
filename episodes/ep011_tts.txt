Midjourney — the king of aesthetic quality — finally launched their API in late 2025 after years of being Discord-only. It's still relatively new as a developer tool, and access requires a subscription plan.

Flux, from Black Forest Labs, is the open-source disruptor. Their FLUX.2 model family offers both a hosted API and self-hosted options. If you care about control and cost at scale, Flux is the one to watch.

Midjourney still leads in pure aesthetics. If you need marketing visuals, product shots, or anything that needs to look polished and artistic, Midjourney's output is hard to beat. It has a distinctive style that people recognize — and love.

DALL-E 3 excels at instruction following. Tell it exactly what you want — specific text in the image, precise layouts, detailed scenes — and it delivers more accurately than the others. The new gpt-image-1 model takes this even further by leveraging GPT's language understanding.

Flux sits in an interesting middle ground. FLUX.2 Pro produces photorealistic images that rival Midjourney, with better prompt adherence than earlier Flux models. And FLUX.2 Dev, the open-weight version, lets you fine-tune on your own data — something neither DALL-E nor Midjourney offers.

Now the part developers really care about — cost.

DALL-E 3 charges per image. Standard quality at 1024 by 1024 runs about four cents per image. HD quality doubles that. The new gpt-image-1 is pricier — roughly eight to twelve cents depending on resolution. It's simple and predictable, but it adds up fast at scale.

Midjourney's API pricing is tied to their subscription plans. The Basic plan at ten dollars a month gives you around two hundred images. Pro at thirty dollars gets you unlimited relaxed generations. For API access specifically, you're looking at roughly five to ten cents per image on the Standard plan. Not cheap, but the quality justifies it for many use cases.

Flux is where it gets interesting for cost-conscious developers. Through the BFL API, FLUX.2 Pro runs about five cents per image. But here's the kicker — you can self-host FLUX.2 Dev for free. Midjourney's API is newer and still maturing. It uses a webhook-based async pattern — you submit a job, get a callback when it's done. Fine for batch processing, but adds complexity for real-time applications.

Flux's API is also async — submit, poll, retrieve. But because it's open source, you have options. Use the hosted API for convenience, or deploy it yourself with full control over the pipeline. Libraries exist for Python, Node, and most major frameworks.
